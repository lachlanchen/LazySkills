# Clean Paragraph Rewrite Loop

Use this reference when prose is technically clear but dense, strange, shallow, boring, AI-like, overpolished, mechanically plain, or no longer belongs to the intended novel.

## Reset Authority

1. Preserve the rejected draft and its critique as diagnostic evidence.
2. Declare whether old events remain canon. Do not leave continuity ambiguous.
3. Rebuild orientation from author-written sources, current steering, accepted canon, and abstract craft study.
4. Treat generated legacy as evidence of failure patterns, not voice authority.

## Define One Paragraph's Burden

Keep the whole-book promise visible, but assign the paragraph one dramatic burden. Require one meaningful change in knowledge, desire, relationship, power, risk, cost, or available path.

Do not require every paragraph to contain the book's history, philosophy, romance, conflict, and speculative premise. Let those forces shape the choice without naming them.

## Draft And Challenge

1. Ask the persistent novelist for one complete candidate.
2. Save it before review.
3. Run a whole-story critic and a first-read prose critic independently.
4. Ask critics for quoted evidence and repair instructions, not substitute prose.
5. If rejected, return both artifacts to the same novelist and request a complete rewrite.
6. Save each revision and repeat only the gates affected by the change.
7. Stop at the revision cap rather than accepting a weak paragraph.

## Watch The Revision Pendulum

### Overcomposed density

Reject a paragraph when every sentence tries to be memorable. Common signs include several relatives introduced for commentary, stacked jokes, image catalogues, moral asides, multiple mystery cues, and a closing sentence that explains the emotional effect.

Keep only the strongest human action and one or two supporting details. Let ordinary connective prose carry the rest.

### Lifeless correction

Reject a revision that removes ornament by turning the scene into orderly summary. Common signs include repeated subject-verb declarations, anonymous camps of opinion, grave uniform tone, functional dialogue, and facts that arrive without appetite, irritation, affection, embarrassment, or subtext.

Restore one specific relational habit or modest countercurrent of warmth. Do not restore a catalogue of effects.

### Late-stage overediting

When a candidate has life and critics identify one clear antecedent, repeated word, or spatial problem, fix only that obstacle. Do not reopen imagery, theme, or structure merely because another rewrite is possible.

## Preserve Blank Space

Put meaning into what a person does instead of narrating what the action means. A returned bowl can carry care, authority, irritation, and evasion without an explanatory sentence.

Permit scene cuts, temporal gaps, and tonal shifts when the reader can still follow the person, immediate desire, and causal pressure. Use ellipses only when the prose wants them, never as a mandatory marker.

Test the distinction:

- **Productive uncertainty:** The reader knows the question but not the answer.
- **Obscurity:** The reader first has to determine what the question refers to.

Keep the first; repair the second.

## Make Scene Cuts Carry Life

A clean cut can cross years or places without an explanatory bridge. After the cut, establish a concrete present purpose before reviving mystery: a delivery, shift, meal, debt, promise, journey, or person waiting. Let the larger story interrupt a life that was already moving.

When an image or place recurs, give it a new practical use. A road remembered in a family tale may later be a working detour. This creates resonance without announcing symbolism.

Test the cut:

- the reader knows whose life has resumed;
- the person wanted something before the plot arrived;
- time, money, labor, affection, or obligation makes interruption costly;
- recurring scenery works physically in the present scene;
- the next question arises from collision, not from an explanatory hook.

Do not force ellipses, timestamps, or transition paragraphs. Use them only when they improve the prose's natural movement.

## Use The Smallest Evidence Of Danger

One missing shoe or one remnant of restraint may be stronger than a forensic catalogue of mud, torn clothing, wounds, posture, breath, and ominous behavior. Keep only the details a person would notice first and that the next action needs.

Withhold cause, captor, route, and history when those are the true questions. Do not withhold the speaker, physical position, or immediate action. Stop once recognition or choice has changed the scene.

## Translate After Acceptance

Use a separate persistent translator only after both source-language gates pass. Pass accepted prose, nearby continuity, names, and a small glossary.

Run an independent target-language gate. Search for:

- translated syntax and formal diction in intimate scenes;
- repeated kinship explanation;
- long attributive chains;
- unnatural child or family speech;
- stiff handling of bowls, clothing, doors, food, and bodies;
- distorted routes or spatial relations;
- facts added to smooth the sentence;
- explanation that removes source-language blank space.

Also reject incident-report prose: stacked location verbs, repeated state checks, conspicuous classifiers, bureaucratic noun order, and sentences that explain every object's position. A factually clear translation can still feel dead. Rebuild sentence movement and spoken rhythm instead of replacing flagged words one by one.

Revise and re-review until the target prose reads naturally on its own.

## Persist Acceptance

Store:

- every source candidate;
- each whole-story and prose review;
- every translation candidate and target-language review;
- the accepted bilingual paragraph;
- continuity cautions for the next paragraph.

Do not publish a partial chapter as a finished reader artifact. Render and share the clean chapter PDF only after an earned chapter close.
